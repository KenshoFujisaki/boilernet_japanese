Lawyers for Alberta premier threaten blogger who owns edstelmach.ca

Tue, 2008-01-08 18:35.

By: Jim Macdonald, THE CANADIAN PRESS

EDMONTON - Lawyers for Alberta Premier Ed Stelmach are accusing a young blogger of "misappropriating" Stelmach's personality after the university student bought the rights to the Internet domain edstelmach.ca.

Dave Cournoyer, host of the political website daveberta, paid $14 for the rights to the domain name last April.

Cournoyer got a letter from a Calgary law firm in early December accusing him of registering Stelmach's domain name in bad faith and interfering with the premier's image.

"This constitutes an invasion and impairment of our client's exclusive right to market his personality," says the letter from the law firm Walsh, Wilkins, Creighton.

"Our client is entitled to the amount he would reasonably have received in the market for the permission to use his name."

Stelmach's lawyers want Cournoyer to hand over a share of advertising revenues from his site and give the premier the rights to the domain name.

"It seems a little bizarre that the premier's first reaction would be to sue a 24-year-old university student and blogger," Cournoyer said Tuesday.

"I don't take well to threats from politicians, so I'm going to be seeking advice from a lawyer on what my options are."

Stelmach is on holidays and unavailable for comment and officials in the premier's office refused to comment.

Jim Campbell, executive-director of the Alberta Tory party, says it's reasonable for Stelmach to want control of how his name is used.

"I don't think it's embarrassing to the premier or the party," said Campbell. "The premier has taken action as a private citizen to protect his good name and how it is used."

Cournoyer says he was surprised the Tory party or government communications staff didn't purchase the domain name after Stelmach won the Tory leadership and became premier a little more than a year ago.

Campbell concedes this was a mistake.

"That was probably an oversight on our part," he said in an interview. "I wish we were perfect, but we're not."

"However, saying that the premier or the party should have registered all possible domain names is kind of tantamount to blaming the victim."

Internet users who logged onto edstelmach.ca were initially directed to the daveberta site, which generates about $20 per month in advertising revenue.

Cournoyer says it's ridiculous that Stelmach's lawyers are seeking a share of the profits from the site, which offers pointed commentary about political events in Alberta.

The blogger says there was no intention to earn a profit when he purchased the domain name, but he's keeping his options open.

"I do have a $5,000 tuition bill and a large sum of student debt that would be nice to be paid off," he said. "But it's a little too early to talk about that right now."

Lewis Klar, a law professor at the University of Alberta, says Cournoyer may be on thin ice when it comes to defending using the premier's name to hub Internet traffic to his website.

"It's basically taking someone else's good will without their permission," said Klar. "Most people would think that someone should be able to use someone else's name to attract attention."

After Cournoyer got the letter from Stelmach's lawyers, he changed the link so that Internet traffic was no longer directed to his own site.

Now web users who log on to edstelmach.ca are linked to the Wikipedia site with the biography of Harry Strom, the Social Credit premier who also had a solid majority when he took office in 1968.

Political analysts have often compared Stelmach to Strom, who was defeated in 1971 by Peter Lougheed, the first of the Tory premiers who have governed Alberta ever since.
