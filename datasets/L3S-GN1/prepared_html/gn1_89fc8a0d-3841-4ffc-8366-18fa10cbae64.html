Nicole Pratt, 34, retires after loss to Nadia Petrova

Article from:
  • Font size: Decrease Increase
  • Email article: Email
  • Print article: Print

Dave Donaghy

January 16, 2008 12:00am

AN EMOTIONAL Nicole Pratt broke down last night after announcing her immediate retirement from tennis.

Pratt, 34, made the snap decision to retire after suffering a crushing 6-1 6-3 loss to big-hitting Russian 14th seed Nadia Petrova in the first round.

After wrestling with the decision for more than a month, Pratt, who was contesting her 18th Open, finally pulled the pin on her career.

"Yep, this is it. Yep, last Australian Open, last match," Pratt said.

"It's an emotional day. I basically have no regrets. I pretty much did all I could. I gave it everything I had."

Pratt said injury battles -- she had knee surgery in October -- had left her with no choice.

"I can't compete against those players with one and a half legs," said Pratt, who plans to take on a coaching role on the WTA Tour.

"I always said to myself if I can't go out there and compete against the best players in the world, and feel like I can win, then I'm not interested in continuing to play. Today, that (loss) told me it was time."

While one veteran bowed out permanently, a new face emerged, with former Russian Anastasia Rodionova.

Playing for Australia for the first time after learning she could last week, Rodionova bucked the trend for the locals yesterday by reaching the second round.

Rodionova, 25, became the fourth Australian woman to emerge from the opening round -- joining Alicia Molik, Casey Dellacqua and Jessica Moore -- after beating German qualifier Angelika Bachmann 7-6 (7-3) 6-4.

Rodionova's win was a rare highlight for the Australian women on day two, with Pratt, as well as wildcards Sophie Ferguson and Christina Wheeler, making exits.

Ferguson, battling a persistent knee injury, lost to Switzerland's Timea Bacsinszky 6-4 6-3, with Wheeler succumbing to Italian Maria Elena Camerin 6-1 6-2.

Molik, Dellacqua and Moore all play second-round matches today -- against seeded opponents.

Molik meets 12th seed Nicole Vaidisova, Casey Dellacqua battles Patty Schnyder, seeded 15th, while Moore, a 17-year-old schoolgirl, faces 17th seed Shahar Peer of Israel.

Pratt said she felt it was time for the next generation of Australian players to step up.

"I think we've been doing well, probably the last year or two, with Sam (Stosur) and Alicia stepping up," she said.

"Unfortunately, illness has hit both players. So, really, I see it as now it is time for some of the young players to step up. I'll be giving up my spot in the Fed Cup team for hopefully a young player to step up."

Ferguson, who is based in Sydney, said the tyranny of distance was having an impact on the hopes of promising Australian players.

"In Italy, they have a tournament every single week, where in Australia we have maybe six, or eight, female tournaments (a year)," said Ferguson, who will continue to work with Tony Roche and Rachel McQuillan this year.

"It's hard at times when you're away and not playing well, but if you've got a good support that helps you get through."
