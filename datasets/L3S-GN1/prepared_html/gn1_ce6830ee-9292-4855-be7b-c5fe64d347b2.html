BAGHDAD (AP) - Secretary of State Condoleezza Rice congratulated Iraq's leader on Tuesday for «quite remarkable» progress toward national reconciliation.

But senior Iraqi officials said Rice also warned Prime Minister Nouri al-Maliki against losing a «golden opportunity» during President Bush's final year in office to bring Sunni Arabs into a unity government.

The officials said Rice

further warned al-Maliki and other Iraqi leaders she met in Baghdad that the recent reduction of violence in Iraq could prove fleeting if the country's main Shiite, Sunni Arab and Kurdish groups did not reach an enduring agreement on the future shape of Iraq.

Underlining the continuing fragility of the security gains made over the past six months, at least five mortars landed late Tuesday in the U.S.-protected Green Zone, home to the U.S. Embassy and the Iraqi government offices. There were no immediate reports of casualties or damage to the heavily fortified central Baghdad region where Rice held meetings several hours earlier.

Rice, who split off from U.S. President George W. Bush's Mideast tour for an unannounced visit to Baghdad, said the Iraqi parliament's approval Saturday of a U.S.-sought benchmark law allowing low-ranking members of Saddam Hussein's Baath party to reclaim government jobs or pensions was a first step and showed that last year's «surge» of American forces was paying dividends.

«It is clearly a step forward for national reconciliation _ a step forward for healing the wounds of the past, and it will have to be followed up by implementation that is in the same spirit of national reconciliation,» she told a news conference after meeting al-Maliki.

«I hope we will focus on what needs to be done, but also on how much has been done,» said Rice, who flew to Baghdad from Riyadh, Saudi Arabia.

Although national reconciliation «has not always moved as fast as some of us sitting in Washington would like, it has certainly moved and, given the legacy, history and stains of tyranny, it has been quite remarkable,» she said.

Three Iraqi officials said Rice told al-Maliki he must take advantage of Bush's last year in the White House to aggressively pursue national reconciliation, with the return of Sunni Arabs ministers to his Cabinet as a top priority.

Bush has vigorously supported al-Maliki since the Iraqi leader took office in May last year. Bush's Democratic critics, however, have not been as supportive, with some charging that al-Maliki lacked the leadership qualities needed to make difficult decisions to reconcile Iraqis.

The Iraqis officials spoke to The Associated Press on condition of anonymity because they are not authorized to share the information with the media. Two of the three attended meetings Tuesday between Rice and Iraqi leaders. The third was briefed by a senior participant.

Rice's emphasis on national reconciliation comes at a time when the embattled al-Maliki appears unable to hold together a functioning government that represents all Iraqis.

Six Sunni Arab ministers quit al-Maliki's government in August to protest his perceived sectarian policies. Kurds remain in the government, but differences have emerged between the two sides over a draft legislation to divvy up Iraq's oil revenue and Kirkuk, the oil-rich city claimed by the Kurds. The city's Arab and Turkomen inhabitants dispute the Kurdish claim.

Al-Maliki has balked at conditions set by the Sunni Arab parliamentary bloc that pulled its ministers from his Cabinet. He threatened to fill the vacant posts with members of Sunni groups that rose against al-Qaida in Anbar province and elsewhere in Iraq and joined U.S. and Iraqi forces in the fight against the terror network.

Al-Maliki, however, never carried out his threat and, furthermore, antagonized many of the Sunni groups by blocking their integration into the Shiite-dominated security forces.

«The Prime Minister told her that the air has been cleared and that the Sunni ministers were expected to return to the government in the near future,» one of the three Iraqi officials said.

The dispute between al-Maliki and the Sunni Arabs has been characterized by public clashes between al-Maliki and Vice President Tariq al-Hashemi, a Sunni Arab whose three-party alliance in parliament _ the Iraqi Accordance Front _ pulled out its six ministers in August.

The two met Monday for the first time in months after a mediation by top politicians, including President Jalal Talabani, a Kurd, Vice President Adel Abdul-Mahdi, a Shiite, and Abdul-Aziz al-Hakim, leader of the country's largest Shiite party. Rice met all three Tuesday.

«We see a desire by the government to meet our demands,» said Salim Abdullah, a Sunni lawmaker and a spokesman for the Accordance Front. «Monday's meeting narrowed differences and created a climate of trust between al-Hashemi and al-Maliki.



The return of the Sunnis to the government could go a long way in restoring the leadership credentials of al-Maliki, but should not be taken as the magic key to national reconciliation.

Leaders of the Sunni groups fighting alongside the U.S. military against al-Qaida have made no secret of their political ambitions with some claiming that they, rather than traditional Sunni parties, are the true representative of the Sunni Arab community.

The de-Baathification law that Rice congratulated al-Maliki on is one of 18 steps which Washington considers benchmarks to promoting reconciliation.

A senior aide to al-Maliki said Rice also encouraged the prime minister to promote the progress of the other benchmark legislation, including one regulating provincial elections, constitutional amendments and legislation on the equitable distribution of oil revenue.

In northern Iraq on Tuesday, Turkish warplanes bombed Kurdish rebel hideouts, the Turkish military announced, as Turkey's deputy military commander made a surprise visit to Baghdad to discuss cooperation in fighting the rebels.

Gen. Ergin Saygun, the Turkish military's second-in-command, met with Iraqi Lt. Gen. Nasier Abadi, deputy chief of staff for the Iraqi army, as well as with Gen. David Petraeus, the top U.S. commander in Iraq, the military said in a separate statement.

They discussed fighting Kurdish rebels and possible military cooperation, the army said.

It was a first visit to Iraq by a top Turkish commander since the U.S.-led invasion in 2003.

Tuesday's Turkish air raid was the fourth on rebel positions in northern Iraq since the military launched a bombing campaign on Dec. 16. Prime Minister Recep Tayyip Erdogan vowed on Monday to «finish» the rebels soon.

The Turkish bombardment started at about 11:30 a.m. and was still going on, said Zirar Mohammed, a top administrative official in the Iraqi border town of Sedakanil and Col. Hussein Tamir of the border forces in the city of Dahuk.
